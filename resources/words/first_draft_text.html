A Different Kind of Honey: Redefining Family Values in A Taste of Honey

             This paper aims to show that A Taste of Honey explores the potential harm caused by traditional family values, especially for those born into unfortunate circumstances, as portrayed by Jo and Helen. Instead, the play presents an alternative family structure with Jo and Geoffrey, highlighting the possibility of finding love and support beyond conventional expectations.

             I argue that Jo’s only one family Helen's behavior not just legally and financially binds Jo, as a child who should receive parental protection, but also mentally constrains Jo, who has artistic and emotional sensibility with relationship, as shown that she paints people’s portraits.

             Helen, depicted as an ill-equipped parent, harbors negative beliefs about relationships considering them as a zero-sum game. She believes that if someone receives more love and attention, others will receive less. This mindset is described when she uses the term “jealous” to Jo when Peter arrives Helen-Jo’s home and talks with Helen. Helen believes that Jo must be jealous about having no attention, not considering that Jo is anxious and uncomfortable because of Peter who may be intruder.

             That Helen’s limited view of love leads her to offend Geffrey and drive him away, when she came back to Jo depending on biological daughter, Helen’s last one relationship after breaking up with Peter. As a result, she intervenes and spoils Jo’s relationship ruining her daughter’s chance at happiness without her mother.

              Helen, with her narrow-mindedness in love and cares, never care about Jo whenever she focused on another relationship, such as with her boyfriend Peter. This careless leads Jo to experience a great deal of misery and confusion. In the first scene of Helen and Jo moving to a new house, Helen does not warm the house for her cold daughter, and just drink for warm her own body, which means a lack of care for Jo from her parent.

             Jo’s confusion gets even deeper when Helen marries Peter and leave her young daughter alone. Leaving alone with familial absence and loneliness, causing Jo to question her heredity and even her sanity, as described asking her real father and doubt herself if she is mad. (Act 1, Scene 2).

             In contrast with these dark moments, Jo finds peace and freedom with their open-mindedness with Geoffrey, even if she could be anxious about becoming a mother. Therefore, A Taste of Honey suggests that Jo’s life with Geoffrey is better than living with Helen.

             Though the family formed by Jo and Geoffrey cannot be a perfect family because of no traditional marriage or blood-relations like Helen and Jo, and they could lose each other anytime, it still offers them the opportunity to experience love and support. Despite the challenges they face as a single mother and a gay man, their partnership provides a stable environment, free from judgment. Jo and Geoffrey demonstrate that a non-traditional family can still offer love and partnership, with each taking on the roles of mother and father despite their unique circumstances.

             In conclusion, A Taste of Honey presents the contrast between the traditional concept of family, as represented by Helen and Jo, and the more progressive idea of family based on emotional bonds as represented by Jo and Geoffrey. The story demonstrates family connections could be found over traditional boundaries, allowing for a broader and more inclusive definition of family.
